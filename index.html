Live results — auto-updating bracket

The bracket and the title-odds simulation update themselves. On load, and every few minutes after, the page pulls completed knockout results from ESPN's public FIFA World Cup feed (their undocumented but open site.api.espn.com scoreboard — no key, read straight from your browser), locks each winner into its bracket slot, marks the loser out, drops the decided tie from the Matches and Parlay lists, and re-runs the simulation so every downstream number reflects who has actually advanced. It cascades — once a round's results are in, the next round's matchups are known and get filled as those finish too. The badge under the header shows the sync state. If the feed is ever unreachable, the page silently falls back to the results baked into the file, so it can never show a broken bracket — worst case it's as current as the last save. The ratings and the model itself don't auto-update (those are built from group-stage data), only the knockout results do.

What this is

A value tool for the World Cup knockout rounds. For each match it shows two independent models — our own Elo-based line and a third-party feed — side by side, converts our number to a fair (no-vig) price, and once you type in your book's prices returns the expected value of each side, the vig, and a ★ wherever both models beat the market on the same side. Two models plus the market agreeing is a far stronger flag than any one alone.

Our model: Elo → goals → prices

Built in two pieces, each from the source that's best for the job. Who's favoured comes from the World Football Elo gap, turned into a result expectancy (the eloratings winning-expectancy curve) and split into win/draw/win — so the result line rides the trusted strength rating, not a 3-game form blip. How many goals comes from each team's attack and defence (goals scored and conceded per game, regressed toward the mean for the short sample) — that sets the match total, which feeds a Poisson scoreline grid. From that one grid we read 1X2, Over/Under 2.5, both-teams-to-score, and the likely scorelines. So Elo fixes the side; the goal data fixes the total; everything else falls out consistently. The expected-goals line under each bar (e.g. 1.92–1.13) is what the model actually thinks will happen.

The feed, and the gap

The Feed column is a separate third-party match model — your cross-check, not gospel (treat it like the UFC finish-rate model). When our Elo line and the feed disagree by 8+ points a model-gap note appears: that's where to dig, because one of the two is wrong and the market may be sitting on the wrong one. Worth knowing why they can diverge: the feed appears to weight current-tournament form harder (it has Germany well over our Elo line), and it likely carries home-venue for the three hosts — which our model deliberately doesn't (see limits). On a USA/Mexico/Canada home match, trust the feed's lean more. The board at the top of the Matches tab ranks every result by that gap, so the largest model-vs-market disagreements across the slate sit in one place — a triage list, not a buy list. Below it, the live-underdogs board flips to the dog's perspective: for every fed tie it takes the lower-rated side and ranks by how much more our model likes the underdog's win than the price does, with the dog's advance% (extra time and shootout included) alongside. Positive rows are where a live dog might be underpriced — the spots to pull a real number and check.

Honest limits of our model

The Matches tab is neutral site, so the hosts (USA, Mexico, Canada) are under-rated there on days they play at home — use the Matchup Lab's home toggle to add the bump (below). Attack/defence rest on 3–4 games and are noisy even after shrinking, so treat Over/Under and BTTS as directional, not precise; knockout games also trend lower-scoring than the group stage these totals were built on, so Overs are if anything generous. Independent Poisson slightly under-counts draws and 1-1/0-0, so the draw and BTTS-no fair prices are mildly conservative. The 1X2 line is 90 minutes; the separate to-advance line adds extra time and penalties (below). For lopsided mismatches (a top seed vs a minnow), the Elo gap can demand a bigger winning margin than the modelled goal total naturally supports; the model floors each side's goal rate at a small positive value so the distribution stays valid (no impossible >100% or negative prices). That nudges the modelled total for those blowouts slightly upward — harmless for the win and advance numbers, and directionally reasonable, since heavy favourites tend to run up the score.

To advance — extra time & penalties

A knockout that's level after 90 goes to 30 minutes of extra time, then a shootout. So advancing isn't the same as winning in 90, and the "To advance" line on each card prices the real market. Extra time is modelled as the same Poisson run at roughly a third of the pace (30 of 90 minutes); if it's still level, the shootout is treated as a coin flip. That last part is deliberate: penalty shootouts are empirically close to random and even good models barely beat 50%, so rather than invent a penalty rating we don't have, we call it 50/50 and flag it as the least certain part of the number. The upshot is useful — a heavy favourite's advance number sits well above its 90-minute win % (France 85% → ~93% to advance), while a slight favourite barely clears 50% because the draw mass resolves near-evenly (Mexico 36% → ~52%). When the to-advance price implies more certainty than that, there's an edge.

Matchup Lab & the home bump

The Matchup Lab runs the full model on any two teams in the field — look ahead to a bracket matchup that hasn't been drawn, price a hypothetical final, or settle a "who beats who". The home selector adds the standard +100-Elo home-field advantage (eloratings' convention) to one side, which is also the right way to handle the three hosts on home soil. It shows the same W/D/W, to-advance, totals, BTTS and likely scores as a real card, plus the ratings driving the result.

Stake sizer — EV & Kelly

In the Lab, pick any outcome and enter a price to get EV and the Kelly stake. Kelly is the bankroll fraction that maximises long-run growth: f = (p·d − 1)/(d − 1). Full Kelly is the theoretical optimum but swings hard and punishes any model error, so half-Kelly is the standard, sane default — and given this model isn't back-tested yet, even quarter-Kelly is defensible. A non-positive f means no edge: pass.

Scoreline map — correct score & totals

Below the Lab's markets sits the full Poisson scoreline grid for the matchup — home goals down the side, away across the top, every cell shaded by likelihood with the modal score outlined and its exact probability and fair price on hover. It's the same independent-Poisson distribution the 1X2 and totals lines come from, just shown whole, which is what correct-score and scorecast markets price. The totals block reads Over/Under at 1.5, 2.5 and 3.5 off the exact total-goals distribution (a Poisson with mean equal to the two expected-goals numbers added), and the correct-score pricer gives EV on any exact score against your book. Treat correct score as a longshot market — the probabilities are small and the same 3–4-game noise in the attack/defence inputs lands hard on individual cells, so stake light. Beneath it, a total-goals distribution bar chart shows the whole shape of the total — the chance of 0, 1, 2, … goals — with a dashed line marking where the Over/Under 2.5 split falls, so you can see at a glance how much of the mass sits on each side rather than reading it off a single number. Below that, a same-game-parlay correlation matrix shows how the common single-match legs move together, as a correlation coefficient pulled from the same joint grid — green means a pair's true "both happen" chance runs higher than multiplying the legs as if independent (so a book building the SGP off independent prices is leaving value), red means the combo is dearer than it looks. Complementary pairs like Over/Under come out at −1 exactly, as they must, which is the sanity check that the joint probabilities are internally consistent.

Market tools — devig, true edge & arbitrage

The last Lab panel takes the book's prices for any of four markets — match result, to-advance, Over/Under 2.5, or both-teams-to-score — and strips the vig by the proportional method: each price becomes an implied probability (one over decimal odds), those are summed to the overround, and each is divided by that sum so they total 100%. That no-vig line is the market's actual estimate with the margin removed — the sharp number to test a model against. The true edge column is the model probability minus the no-vig probability, which is more honest than model-minus-raw-price because it doesn't credit the model for beating a number that was inflated by hold. The to-advance market is priced against the model's full advance probability (including extra time and the coin-flip shootout), so it's the right comparison for a "to qualify" line. If the prices imply under 100% — which happens when you shop the best of each outcome across books — it's a guaranteed arbitrage, and the panel returns the profit and the stake split that locks an equal return whichever way it lands.

The field map

The scatter atop the Ratings tab plots all 48 teams by chance creation (shot dominance, across) against defensive solidity (GA/90, up — stingier is higher), with brightness scaling to the Power rating. It's the same data as the table, arranged so the shape of the field is legible at a glance: the top-right quadrant holds the sides that both create and prevent, the dashed lines are the field medians, and a team marooned bottom-left is one the model has little faith in at either end. Beneath the scatter, a confederation rollup aggregates the 48-team field by governing body — average Power rating, the strongest qualifier, and how many are still alive in the knockout bracket — a quick read on where the tournament's depth actually sits.

Team comparison radar

In the Matchup Lab, a radar overlays the two chosen sides across five dimensions — Power, Attack (shot dominance), Defence (GA/90 inverted), Possession and Finishing (goals minus expected) — each scaled to the whole 48-team field, so the outer ring is best-in-field and the centre worst. It's a fast read on the shape of a matchup: where one side's polygon pushes past the other's is where its edge lives. Finishing is the noisy axis — a three-game over- or under-performance versus xG that's as likely variance as skill — so weight it lightly.

Portfolio staking — Kelly across a card

Single-bet Kelly sizes one play in isolation; the Staking tab sizes a whole card at once. Enter several plays (model probability auto-fills, you supply the price) and it solves for the stake mix that maximises expected log-growth of the bankroll across all of them simultaneously — a proper concave optimisation, not a rule of thumb. The key result it surfaces: every stake comes out below its solo-Kelly number, because bets that run at the same time share one bankroll and stack variance, so the growth-optimal move is to trim each. It assumes the plays are independent, so combine any same-match legs in the Parlay tab first and bring the single combined price here. It reports each stake as a bankroll fraction and a dollar amount, the total deployed, and the expected growth rate — at quarter, half or full Kelly. Half is the sane default; full is the theoretical max-growth point and swings brutally, doubly so on a model that isn't back-tested. Underneath, a card-outcome table makes the variance concrete: it enumerates all 2ⁿ win/lose combinations of your plays at the stakes shown, weights each by model probability, and reports the expected return, the chance the card profits or loses, and the best- and worst-case swings — the spread you're actually signing up for, in bankroll terms.

Title Odds — the bracket Monte Carlo

The real, locked knockout bracket (verified against FIFA's official match schedule and the third-place-team assignment table) is simulated 20,000 times. Each tie is decided by the model's to-advance probability — 90 minutes, then extra time, then the 50/50 shootout — and winners feed forward round by round to the final. Canada and Brazil have already won their Round-of-32 ties, so they're locked into the Round of 16; the other 14 R32 ties and all of the Round of 16, quarters, semis and final are simulated. The output is each team's chance to reach every round and to lift the trophy, with a no-vig fair price for the title — set that against your book's outrights and the gap is the futures edge. Two honest caveats: the four top seeds (Argentina, Spain, France, England) sit in four separate quarters, so the model's love of Elo gaps concentrates probability on them more than the market will — favourites read short here, longshots long. And six rounds of a neutral-site, no-injuries model compound, so deep-run numbers are a lens, not a lock. Two tools sit on this tab: click any column header to rank the field by that round (useful for "to reach the final" or "to make the semis" markets), and the Outright value panel turns the sim into a bet — pick a team and a futures market, enter your book's price, and read EV plus the Kelly stake straight off the simulated probability. The run is cached, so the numbers hold still as you move between tabs; hit re-run to redraw, and export odds CSV drops the whole table into a spreadsheet. Two more readouts sit lower: knockout-path difficulty ranks every surviving team by the average Power of the opponents on its projected chalk route to the final (read relative to a team's own strength, since the very top seeds sit low for never facing themselves), and draw-region strength shows the average Power of each quarter and half — the quickest way to see why a half holding two giants depresses everyone's title odds inside it.

Interactive bracket

The Bracket tab is the same verified draw as a clickable tree. Tap a team to send them through and the pick flows forward; change an earlier result and everything downstream that depended on it clears automatically. Every tie with both teams known shows the model's to-advance % on each side, so you can see the lean while you build a path — and ★ model favourites autofills the chalk pick at every node (it lands on Argentina), which is the single most-likely path, not a probability-weighted one. Use it to sanity-check a futures ticket: pencil in the bracket you're betting and see which heavy favourites have to fall for it to land. Your picks are saved automatically and survive a refresh, and ⧉ copy share link packs the whole filled bracket into a URL — open it on another device or hand it to someone and the bracket rebuilds exactly.

Pool bracket optimiser

Below the outright tool, if you're in a bracket pool, this solves for the bracket with the highest expected points under your pool's scoring. Set the points per round and a tree dynamic-programming pass over the simulation's reach probabilities returns the optimal bracket exactly — at each game it weighs the points for calling that winner (round weight times the chance that team actually reaches the next round) against committing the team to later rounds, so heavily back-weighted scoring rewards anchoring on title contenders even through a tough early game. It also computes the naive game-by-game chalk bracket and tells you whether the two differ. In this field they usually don't — the Elo favourites are the most likely winners at every round, so chalk is already the expected-points optimum, and the tool says so rather than inventing a contrarian angle. One honest limit: this maximises expected points, which is the goal in a most-points pool; maximising your chance of winning a pool is a different problem that depends on what your opponents pick, which the tool doesn't know.

Futures ticket — all routes vs the gauntlet

The Futures ticket panel prices an outright the right way and shows the path in the same place. Pick a team, a market (win it / reach a given round) and your price: the headline probability and EV use the all-routes number from the simulation — the bet pays whoever they beat, so that, not any single path, is the correct price. Below it, the gauntlet lists the specific opponents they'd have to get through (your penciled bracket where you've drawn it, the model's favourite elsewhere) with the to-advance % at each step and the toughest one flagged. The product of those steps — "clearing this exact gauntlet" — is almost always lower than the all-routes number, and that gap is the point: a team also wins the trophy when its rivals knock each other out before they ever meet, so its real title equity beats the odds of running the murderers' row of top seeds in sequence. Read the all-routes figure as the price and the gauntlet as the texture of how the run actually has to go.

Which upset helps most

The ⚖ which upset helps most button answers the natural follow-up for any futures position: which single result elsewhere would move your number most? It runs a second simulation and splits the outcomes by whether each rival survives its next game, then reports the lift to your team's number when that rival goes out — ranked, with the biggest obstacle flagged. The lifts are real conditional probabilities, not guesses: for Argentina to win it all the board reads France and Spain at the top (the giants in the far half they'd meet in a final), and for France to reach the final it's Spain (their projected semi). It's most informative on deep markets — a team's Round-of-16 number barely depends on anyone else, and the tool will say so. Treat it as a hedging and rooting guide: the games to watch, and the rival whose deep run is quietly the biggest threat to your ticket.

Final hedge — lock or ride

Holding an outright? The Final hedge panel (it appears once you've got a team and the title market loaded) plans the classic futures lock: if your team reaches the final, a stake on the other side guarantees the same payout whoever wins. Enter your stake and the price you took, pick the likely opponent (the model pre-fills its projection) and the price you'd actually get to hedge, and it returns the lay stake h = s·a/b, the locked profit, and the EV of letting it ride for comparison. The verdict is the honest part: a hedge only adds expected value if the price on the opponent beats fair — at a fair price, locking and riding have identical EV and the hedge simply trades the swing for certainty; at a vig'd price, locking costs you EV, which is fine if you want the sure thing but isn't free money. It tells you which of the three you're looking at, in dollars.

Line-movement tracker

A small logger in the Calibrate tab: record a market's opening price and its price now, and it shows the shift in implied probability — vig included, so it's the raw market move rather than a no-vig figure. A price firming (implied probability rising) is money arriving on that side; drifting is money leaving. It isn't a model output, it's a record of what the market is doing, and sharp money moving a line against your model number is exactly the kind of disagreement worth pausing on. It saves to your browser like the prediction log.

Defence & possession columns

GA/90 (goals allowed per 90) and Poss% come straight from FBref's squad goalkeeping and standard tables — real, not modelled. Green GA/90 = stingy (≤0.70), red = leaky (≥2.0). Useful for knockout totals and clean-sheet reads, where Mexico and Spain (0.00) sit a world apart from Iraq and Tunisia (4.00).

Shot dominance & luck (the underlying lens)

Shot dom is the SoT ratio — your shots on target ÷ (yours + opponents'). Above 50% means you're creating more clear chances than you allow (Argentina 88%, Spain 84% lead). Luck (G−xGp) is goals scored-minus-conceded versus a transparent shot proxy (xGp = 0.30·SoT + 0.035·other shots): gold = banking more than the shots justify (riding finishing/variance — fade candidates), green = creating more than the scoreline shows (unlucky — back candidates). It's the same over/under-performance read you run on UFC finish rates.

Honest limit on the proxy

This is shot volume + on-target rate, not real xG — no shot locations, so a tap-in and a 30-yarder count the same on the off-target term. It's tournament aggregate over 3 games (small sample), and because it's aggregate, it does not feed back into the Power rating — Power is the World Football Elo and Shot dom / Luck sit beside it as a separate lens. Per-match xG would let a model fold them into a rating directly; FBref still isn't carrying it for this event.

The math

American → decimal: +a → 1+a/100, −a → 1+100/a. EV per $1 on an outcome with model probability p at decimal d: EV = p·d − 1. Fair price is 1/p. Vig on a 3-way is Σ(1/d) − 1. No-vig market probability is each 1/d rescaled to sum to 1 — that's the number to compare against the model.

Honest limits

This is a pre-match snapshot, not a pick. Our Elo→Poisson model is transparent but not back-tested yet — so treat it as decision support, not proof of edge, and use the Calibrate tab to actually score it against the closing line as results land. The full model limits (neutral site, directional totals, the favourite concentration in Title Odds, the 50/50 shootout) are spelled out in the sections above. Live and just-finished matches are excluded from value. Shop your own books — the edge lives in beating the price shown — and method/round-specific soccer props carry heavy vig that a boost rarely overcomes, same lesson as the parlays.
